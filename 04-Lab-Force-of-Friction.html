Lab 04 Instructions - Force Of Friction

Note: This lab is fairly involved. Please plan your time accordingly. There are video instructions at the bottom of this page which explain all of the details of this experiment. This text is a brief overview of steps involved:

In this exercise, you will be using two separate experiments to calculate the coefficient of friction between a plywood board and a toy Einstein doll. In the first experiment, the doll is placed on an inclined plane and allowed to begin sliding. In the second experiment, the doll is placed in a Gravitron while the rotational velocity is decreased until the doll slides.

When you finish the lab you will be able to:

  • Draw Free Body Diagrams and write out Newton’s laws for each experiment
  • Calculate the mathematical expression for the coefficient of friction \(\mu\)
  • Perform error analysis on your final result

The assignment will conclude with an explanation of how to approach writing an abstract which displays your findings. You will then write and submit your own abstract for the experiments.

Inclined Plane Experiment

The first experiment shows a plywood board being inclined until the block slides. You need to use this experiment to calculate the coefficient of friction. The only information you will need is the angle of inclination \(\theta\).

  1. Use the video to observe when the doll starts sliding. This angle MUST BE measured in radians, not degrees (don’t forget to change the mode on your calculator) . You should record the angle that the doll starts sliding as well as an estimation of the error in your measurement.
  2. Create a Free Body Diagram (FBD) for the inclined plane, with the three forces present as well as a coordinate system (I would recommend using a rotated coordinate system, similar to lecture and the text).
  3. Use the FBD and Newton’s second law to write a system of equations for the force acting on the block
  4. The final result should be:

\[\mu = \tan ( \theta )\]

  1. Perform error analysis to find the error in your measurement. Please see the supplemental worksheet Experimental Methods: Propagation of error for an explanation of the error calculation. The final result is

\[E(\mu) =\sqrt{\left( \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial \theta} \right) ^2 E[\theta]^2} = \frac{E[\theta]}{\cos^2(\theta)}\]

Example calculation

  • \(\theta\): the measurement of the angle when the block starts to slide down the ramp (for this experiment theta is somewhere around 25 degrees, which is 0.4363 radians)
  • \(E[\theta]\): the measurement uncertainty in the quantity theta. This uncertainty must be estimated by the experimenter using the Pivot video. How accurate do you think you are with your measurements (to one standard deviation)? Do you think the error in measurement is 1 degree? or 0.1 degree? or 10 degrees? I think the error is somewhere around 1 degree (which is 0.01745 radians) for this experiment. Please estimate this error for yourself, and use a different value if you think 1 degree is not accurate.

Now use these values to ’estimate’ the error in \(\mu\) (remember to convert angles to radians and change your calculator to radian mode):

\[\mu = \tan(\theta) = \tan(0.4363) = 0.466\] \[E[\mu] = \frac{1}{\cos^2(\theta)} E[theta] = 1/(cos(0.4363)^2 * 0.4363 = 0.0212\]

Your final answer would then be quoted as: “\(\mu = 0.466 \pm 0.0212\)”

Gravitron Experiment

The second experiment uses a spinning device known as a gravitron. As the device spins, the centripetal acceleration creates a Normal force which holds the block against the inside of the plywood block. As the Gravitron slows, the centripetal acceleration decreases, and therefore the Normal force decreases and the friction is not able to hold the figurine up.

Part 1:

  1. Use the top view of the gravitron to measure the angle every 0.25 seconds. Record this data into the table on Pivot and create a plot of the position as a function of time. This figure also includes a polynomial fit of the data that you will use in the next steps.
  2. Use the path length equation (\(x = r \theta\)) to convert the angular measurement (\(\theta\)) to position measurement (\(x\))
  3. Make a measurement of the time which the figure falls and record this value (with estimated error).
  4. Use the fit parameters to calculate the velocity of the figurine at the above time. The quadratic fit has statistical estimation of error included in the fit parameters

\[x(t) = x_i + v_i \, t + \tfrac12 a\, t^2 \qquad \rightarrow \qquad y = Ax^2 + Bx +C\]

\[v(t) = v_i + a \, t = B + 2 \, A \, t\]

  1. Perform error analysis to find the error in your measurement. Again, refer to the Experimental Methods: Propagation of error worksheet for an explanation of the error equation. The final result is

\[E[v] = \sqrt{(E[B])^2 + (2 \, t \, E[A])^2 + (2 \, A \, E[t])^2}\]

Part 2:

  1. Create a Free Body Diagram (FBD) of the figurine on the gravitron, with the 3 forces present along with the coordinate system used.
  2. Use the FBD and Newton’s second law to write a system of equations for the force acting on the figurine.
  3. The final result should look like

\[\mu = \frac{gr}{v^2}\]

  1. Perform error analysis to find the error in your measurement. Please see the video for an explanation of the error equation. The final result is

\[E[\mu] = \sqrt{ \left(\frac{r}{v^2} E[g] \right)^2 + \left( \frac{g}{v^2} E[r] \right)^2 + \left( \frac{2gr}{v^3}E[v] \right)^2 }\]

Writing an Abstract

The final part of this lab includes writing an abstract for submission to Canvas. This is a critical skill for a professional scientist or engineer. Writing short and descriptive paragraphs of your work will be used when introducing results to colleagues, coworkers and supervisors.

The abstract should be very short (4-6 sentences in total), and should be filled with details about the experiment. The main purpose of an abstract is to explain what is contained in the rest of the report. For this course, focus on the physics involved and the measurement taken. This does not include set-up, procedure, and analysis.

When writing your abstract, please focus on answering these four questions:

  1. Purpose
    1. What physics were you trying to examine?
    2. Basic concept involved (i.e. Motion of a Damped Pendulum)
  2. Method
    1. How was the experiment designed and performed?
    2. What was measured? (i.e. the angular position)
  3. Results
    1. What were your findings? You MUST include your experimental (measured) results!
    2. Display your most significant results (i.e. the frequency and dampening)
    3. Include your error (i.e. \(5.1 \, rad/s \pm 0.2 \, rad/s\))
  4. Conclusion
    1. Do your results agree with your understanding of theory? If not, what can be improved?

Please avoid writing any of the following in your abstract:

  • Do not show any computer code
  • Omit any intricate details of experimental setup or theory
  • Do not include set-up, procedure, analysis or tables of data.

Creating an abstract - Ballistic motion

To start, please write four separate sentences explaining each of the above questions. By concatenating them together, you will have a rough draft of an abstract. Here is an example for an experiment on free fall motion:

  1. This experiment investigates the effect of gravity on a mass free to fall in two dimensions.
  2. A launcher was used to shoot a ball at a constant initial velocity and the final range of impact was measured.
  3. The theoretically predicted range was found to be \(1.422 \pm 0.091 ~ m\) and the average measured range was \(1.366~m\).
  4. This result shows the kinematic equations can be successfully used to predict the motion of the mass, even when ignoring air resistance.

Now put these sentences together and read the text. It will most likely not make much sense. Please rewrite these sentences to include any essential details, but only include vital information.

This experiment investigates the effect of gravity on a mass free to fall in two dimensions. A spring loaded launcher was used to launch a steel ball at a constant initial velocity and measured the final range when the ball impacts the ground. The theoretically predicted range was found to be \(1.422 \pm 0.091 ~ m\) and the average measured range was \(1.366~m\). This result shows the kinematic equations can be successfully used to predict the motion of the mass, even when ignoring air resistance.

Example abstract - Conical spring oscillations

Here is another abstract for another experiment which you can use as an example of a well written abstract for this course. This abstract is a good example of how you can condense lots of information down into a short paragraph:

We present the measurement and analysis of the motion of a vertical plane spring-mass oscillator assuming simple harmonic motion. We find the spring constant k and the mass distribution constant c of the conical spring used to be \(k=8.107 \pm 0.013 N/m\) and \(c=0.307 \pm .005\) (dimensionless). Using these values, we predict the frequency of the undamped oscillator with a load mass of \(0.299 \pm 0.002~Kg\) to be \(4.824 \pm 0.021~Hz\) and measure the frequency to be \(4.8249 \pm 0.0003 ~ s^{-1}\). This study shows that undamped oscillators display a linear relationship between load mass and squared period, in agreement with theory presented.
