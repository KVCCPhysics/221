0.1. Energy

In the last unit, we studied forces. The solutions were found by writing out Newtons laws and brute forcing to a solution. When studying conservation laws, the interest switches to the summation (or integration) of the force applied over a distance (Energy) or a time (Momentum). This change of focus can then rely on calculus to pre-calculate the change in energy (and momentum) to give a shortcut to a solution.

The pre-calculated shortcut is affectionately known as the “conservation of Energy”.

The definition of a conservative force is built upon a concept called path-independence; where the result of the summation of the force is independent of the path the object takes. A different way of saying this: If the force is ’conservative’, you know the force formula and can simply calculate its sum in advance.

\[W = \int \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{x}\]

Here are some conservative forces:

  \(-dU/dx\) \(\int F\cdot dx\)
Name Force Work (energy)
Kinetic \(\qquad ma \qquad\) \(\qquad \frac12 m v^2 \qquad\)
Gravity \(\qquad mg \qquad\) \(\qquad mgh \qquad\)
Spring \(\qquad -kx \qquad\) \(\qquad \frac12 k x^2 \qquad\)
Electric \(\qquad qE \qquad\) \(\qquad -qV \qquad\)

An clear example of a non-conservative force is friction. Friction is dependent on the path taken (did you go thru the icy patch or go around?) and is also largely unknown because of the reactionary nature of the normal force and surface inconsistencies. The complicated nature of non-conservative forces suggests separation of the summation away from the conservative forces, hence \(W_{nc}\).

The shortcut then states: “The work performed on the system is equal to the sum of all of the energy changes”. When all forces are conservative, \(W_{nc}=0\) and you can calculate one unknown in the energy conservation equation. When a non-conservative force is involved, you are required to solve for the unknown \(W_{nc}\) and cannot solve for anything else.

\[W_{nc} = \Delta K + \Delta U\]

0.2. Momentum

The ’momentum’ is the same “sum of the force”, but now over time rather than distance. Performing this integration will yield the time information which is ignored by the Conservation of Energy. This integration can be done, but is not required for the conservation of momentum.

Instead, the conservation law relies on Newtons 3rd law: Every force must have an equal but opposite reaction force. Try tapping your finger on the table. Your finger feels a force, but the table also feels a force which is opposite. The conservation law relies on this opposite reaction. Because of Newtons 3rd law (action-reaction) the curves must match no matter how complicated the shape of the force function \(F(t)\). Summing each curve (integrating) must yield opposite results. The only way the curves can be mismatched is when there is another external force acting on the system.

Please note how general this thought is: Momentum is conserved, no matter how complicated the interaction, as long as the system is isolated. This concept does not care about complicated energy losses such as vibrations, sound, heat, deformation, matter loss or gain, etc. The Conservation of Energy is not very useful in these situations, but the Conservation of Momentum still applies!

\[\Delta p = \int F_\textnormal{ext} ~ dt\]

0.3. How does this apply to the experiment?

You will be firing a ball from a spring loaded launcher, into a catcher, and then both will rotate upwards until reaching a max height. There are 3 distinct parts to this motion, each of which deserves a set of conservation laws:

Ball launched with a spring (Part 3)

In this motion, the ball scrapes its way down a metal tube in reaction to a spring force. This part of the motion does not conserve energy (sound, heat,friction), and the unknown is required to be \(W_{nc}\) The full system will conserve momentum, but only if the system boundary captures both objects, which are currently the ball, and the earth (launcher fixed to table, table fixed to floor, etc). Restricting the system boundary to be the ball means there will be an external force applied from the ball out of the boundary.

\[\require{cancel}W_{\textnormal{nc}} = \left( \tfrac12 mv_f^2 - \cancel{\tfrac12 mv_i^2} \right) + \left( \cancel{mgy_f} - \cancel{mgy_i} \right) + \left( \cancel{\tfrac12 k x^2_f} - \tfrac12 k x^2_i \right)\] \[\require{cancel}\Delta p = \left( \cancel{m_1 v_{1i}} -m_1 v_{1f} \right) =\int F_{\textnormal{ext}} ~ dt\]

This motion includes a non-conservative force, and thus energy is lost (\(W_{nc}\neq 0\)). Attempts to use the conservation of energy to solve for an unknown like the spring constant will only yield incorrect results. Conservation of Energy is not helpful in lossy (path-dependent) events, as the unknown is already required to be \(W_{nc}\).

We will have to use a different method to find the spring constant \(k\); namely forces:

\[\sum F_y = kx-mg = 0\]

Collision of Ball and Catcher (Part 2)

During the collision, energy is again not conserved. There is a sound which you can clearly hear at the instant the catch happens, unseen thermal energy transfer which heats the objects up, etc. Fortunately the system boundary is now smaller, only the ball and the catcher arm are required (instead of the entire Earth)

The interesting conservation to think through is the force curves cancelling with each other thru this catch. The levers and parts inside of the catcher are asymmetric and the forces would be incredibly complicated, but action-reaction (\(\Delta p=0\)) still applies. (part 2)

\[\require{cancel}W_{\textnormal{nc}} = \left( \tfrac12 m_1v_{f}^2 - \tfrac12 m_1v_{1i}^2 \right) + \left( \tfrac12 m_2v_{f}^2 - \cancel{\tfrac12 m_2v_{2i}^2} \right) + \cancel{\Delta U}\] \[\require{cancel}\Delta p = \cancel{\int F_{\textnormal{ext}} ~ dt} = \left( \cancel{m_1 v_{1i}} -m_1 v_{1f} \right) + \left( \cancel{m_2 v_{2i}} - m_2 v_{2f} \right)\]

Catcher+Ball swings to max height (Part 1)

This is the simplest part of the motion. Note that the force integration can be performed over time (much harder, see oscillations later in the course) or over distance (very simple to write down). Here we will only look at the summation of force over distance, which is known as conservation of energy:

\[\require{cancel}\cancel{W_{\textnormal{nc}}} = \left( \cancel{\tfrac12 mv_f^2} - \tfrac12 mv_i^2 \right) + \left( mgy_f - \cancel{mgy_i} \right) + \left( \cancel{\tfrac12 k x^2_f} - \cancel{\tfrac12 k x^2_i} \right)\] \[\require{cancel}\left( \cancel{m_1 v_{1i}} -m_1 v_{1f} \right) + \left( \cancel{m_2 v_{2i}} - m_2 v_{2f} \right) = \int F_{\textnormal{ext}} ~ dt\]
