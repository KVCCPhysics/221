1.3. Estimate the gravitational acceleration

Now lets look at the math and find an estimate of the gravitaional acceleration. Lets assume that the ball follows the normal kinematic equations: \[x_f = x_i + v_i \, t + \frac12 a\,t^2\] \[v_f = v_i + a\,t\]

The next question is: which variables are known and which are uknown? You have two independent equations, and are allowed to have two unknowns. In this experiment, the position and the time are easy to measure, but the velocity and acceleration are not. We will have to either make estimates of the velocity or figure out a way to avoid the calculation if possible.

Remember that the goal is to measure the acceleration. This means one unknown is already taken (\(a\)). This leaves two unknowns (\(v_i\) and \(v_f\)) where the math requires one. In order to avoid this problem altogether, lets just start collecting data at the very top of the throw (making one variable equal to zero).

1.4. Finding the acceleration numerically

This is a good first approximation, but the video contains a whole lot of data that we are ignoring. Lets use some calculus to find a better approximation to the acceleration:

Method 1: Numerical Derivative

We know calculus! Lets find a numerical derivative. We will use the simple finite difference method for evaluation. In this method, it is assumed that the measurements are uniform with for the independent variable (the one in the denominator). This is the case with this experiment, as the frame rate (frequency) is fixed at \(f=120~fps\), which makes the time interval (period) \(h = \Delta t = \tfrac{1}{f}= \tfrac{1}{120} = 0.008333~sec\)

\[v_i = \frac{dx}{dt} \Rightarrow \frac{(x_i - x_{i-1})}{h}\] \[a_i = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \Rightarrow \frac{\frac{(x_i+1 - x_{i})}{h} - \frac{(x_i - x_{i-1})}{h}}{h} = \frac{x_{i+1} - 2\, x_i + x_{i-1}}{h^2}\]

1.5. Systematic Error

This experiment displays a clear and obvious error: We ignore air resistance! The measurement of the gravitational acceleration will be impacted by this damping and our current theoretical models do not account for this at all. This is a very nice example of a systematic error. This is an error which is unaccounted for (and often overlooked) but will skew the result consistantly in one direction. Please refrain from modifying your theoretical model to account for these errors (motion under constant acceleration for this experiment). The experimental method is designed to test the validity of the theoretical model, and if the model ignores important assumptions, the experiment can hopefully identify the inaccuracies. Please be on the lookout for assumptions that may impact your results systematically and write them down in your lab notes for discussion or consideration later. Some other examples include:

  • throwing the ball at an angle instead of directly upwards
  • misidentifying the time at which the ball has zero vertical velocity
  • try to think of your own!

1.6. Theoretical calculation of \(g\)

The best way to determine the local acceleration due to gravity would be to have a fancy experimental apparatus to measure the accleration in our current location. Unfortunately, having this device is very time/cost/space prohibitive and we must resort to primitive methods such as recording a high speed video of free fall (this is a very poor way to measure \(g\)). But… theory can help us!!

Issac Newton determined exactly how the gravitational force depends on mass and radius (\(F=\tfrac{GmM}{r^2}\)). Using the Earth Graviational Model (EGM2008), the gravitational acceleration in Kalamazoo is calculated to be: \(\vec{g} = (9.80284,0.00006,0.00001)\,m/s^2\), where the components are (radial,west,north). This EGM2008 calculation accounts for many factors, including the non-inertial reference frame due to the spin of Earth, elevation from sea level, and Earth’s oblique and non-uniform shape. This is much more accurate than we can determine using an experiment in a college course.

For the rest of the experiment, lets use this model and define the gravitational acceleration to be:

\[g = 9.8028 \pm 0.0001~m/s^2\]

2.1. Error Propogation

The error in the experimental value can be computed using the following function. The error in a function \(f(A,B,...)\) is given as \(E[f]\) and follows the following expression:

\[E[f] = \sqrt{ \left| \frac{\partial f}{\partial A} \right|^2 E[A]^2 + \left| \frac{\partial f}{\partial B} \right|^2 E[B]^2 + \cdots} \]

  1. The error in h is estimated (try to imagine a Gaussian distribution at the measurement locaiton).

3. Part 3: Predicting ballistic motion

  1. Loosen the bolts on the launcher and reposition the launcher to an incline between \(15^\circ\) and \(25^\circ\).
  2. Measure the vertical distance the ball will drop from the indicated point on the side of the launcher.
  3. Calculate the Range the ball will travel using the initial velocity solved for in Part 1. Again, remember to record your measurement errors.
  4. Place a piece of paper center at your predicted range and tape it to the ground. Do not gauge the range with a test shot. You have predicted the landing location.
  5. Find the instructor before proceeding with the shot.
  6. Measure, in meters, the range of three shots from where the projectile leaves the firing tube to the impact point. Take the average of the three shots.
  7. Compare your average range to your predicted range and calculate the percentage error.

\[ Error = \frac{x_{calc}-x_{ave}}{x_{calc}} * 100 \]
